# Product Requirements Document

## Product

Hackathon Landing Page for "AI VC" Startup Challenge

---

## 1. Overview

This is a landing page for a beginner-friendly one-week software engineering hackathon.

The event challenge is simple: participants build a startup landing page and try to convince an AI acting as a venture capitalist to invest in their startup. The startup can be serious, funny, strange, or absurd. The winner is the team whose website earns the strongest investment result from the AI judge.

The landing page should make the hackathon feel exciting, clear, competitive, and easy to join.

---

## 2. Goal

The landing page should:
- explain the hackathon in a way that people understand immediately
- make the concept feel fun and memorable
- encourage students to register
- answer the main questions without needing extra explanation
- create confidence that the event is organized and worth joining

---

## 3. Target Audience

### Primary audience

Beginner software engineering students in your course.

### Secondary audience
- students who are curious but not very confident
- students who want a fun portfolio project
- students who like design, frontend, AI, or startup culture
- students who may not usually join hackathons

---

## 4. Core Value Proposition

Participants do not need to build a full app. They only need to build a landing page.

That makes the event accessible for beginners, but the twist makes it interesting: their landing page is judged by an AI VC, so they need to make their startup look fundable and persuade the judge.

---

## 5. Product Vision

A landing page that feels modern, playful, and slightly dramatic. It should feel like a startup launch page mixed with a hackathon announcement.

The user should land on the page and within a few seconds understand:
- what the event is
- why it is different
- what they have to build
- when it happens
- how to join

---

## 6. Success Metrics

### Primary metrics:
- number of registrations
- registration conversion rate from page visitors
- number of students who actually submit a project

### Secondary metrics:
- average time spent on page
- click-through rate on registration CTA
- number of questions from students after reading the page
- lower is better here, because the page should answer most things

---

## 7. Main User Story

"As a student, I want to quickly understand the hackathon theme, what I need to build, and how the judging works, so I can decide whether to join."

---

## 8. Secondary User Stories
- "As a beginner, I want to know whether this hackathon is realistic for me."
- "As a participant, I want to understand the challenge and judging criteria clearly."
- "As a student, I want the event to feel fun enough that I actually want to sign up."
- "As a visitor, I want to know the timeline, rules, and submission expectations."

---

## 9. Scope

### In scope
- event overview
- theme explanation
- timeline
- how it works
- judging criteria
- rules and constraints
- registration CTA
- FAQ
- visual style that makes the event feel exciting

### Out of scope
- participant dashboard
- submission portal
- login/auth
- team matching system
- live judging system
- backend-heavy features

---

## 10. Page Objective

The page should persuade students to join the hackathon by making the event look:
- fun
- clear
- achievable
- different from a normal hackathon

---

## 11. Key Message

Build a startup landing page. Convince an AI VC to invest. Get the highest investment score.

---

## 12. Tone and Style

The tone should be:
- playful
- smart
- competitive
- slightly dramatic
- beginner-friendly

It should not feel corporate or overly formal. It should feel like an internet-native event page.

---

## 13. Proposed Page Structure

### 1. Hero section

**Purpose:** Immediate understanding and interest.

**Content:**
- event name
- short one-line pitch
- date or duration
- primary CTA
- secondary CTA

**Example copy:**
- "Build a startup. Convince an AI VC. Get funded."
- "A one-week hackathon where your landing page has one job: make the AI invest."

**Hero should include:**
- strong headline
- short explainer
- registration button
- optional stat or badge like "Beginner-friendly" or "1 week only"

---

### 2. "How it works" section

**Purpose:** Explain the concept in 3 simple steps.

**Content:**
1. Invent a startup
2. Build a landing page for it
3. An AI VC judges whether to invest

This section should make the challenge feel easy to grasp.

---

### 3. Why this hackathon is different

**Purpose:** Sell the twist.

**Content ideas:**
- no full app required
- landing page only
- startup can be serious or ridiculous
- AI is the investor
- strategy matters as much as design

---

### 4. What you need to build

**Purpose:** Make requirements concrete.

**Content:**
- one landing page
- for a startup idea
- polished and complete enough to persuade an AI VC
- any framework allowed
- no backend required unless they want one

---

### 5. Judging criteria

**Purpose:** Show fairness and structure.

**Suggested public criteria:**
- First impression
- Clarity of idea
- Visual quality
- Persuasiveness
- Credibility
- Originality
- Completeness
- Overall fundability

**Optional note:** "The exact judging prompt is private."

---

### 6. Timeline

**Purpose:** Reduce uncertainty.

**Content:**
- registrations open
- kickoff date
- build week
- submission deadline
- judging/demo day
- winners announced

---

### 7. Rules and constraints

**Purpose:** Set boundaries.

**Examples:**
- landing page only
- startup can be fictional
- AI tools allowed
- page must be safe and appropriate
- no broken links to required content
- no malicious tricks or unsafe code
- manipulation is part of the game, but the page must still function as a real landing page

---

### 8. Prize or recognition section

**Purpose:** Boost motivation.

**Examples:**
- highest investment score
- best design
- funniest startup
- most convincing pitch
- people's choice

---

### 9. FAQ

**Purpose:** Handle objections.

**Questions:**
- Do I need to build a full product?
- Can my startup be fake?
- Can I use AI tools?
- Can I work in a team?
- Does the idea have to be serious?
- Do I need backend or authentication?
- Will you reveal the AI prompt?

---

### 10. Final CTA

**Purpose:** End with action.

**Content:**
- reminder of the challenge
- registration button
- deadline reminder

---

## 14. Functional Requirements

The landing page must:
- load as a single-page experience
- work well on desktop and mobile
- include at least one prominent registration CTA above the fold
- include a section clearly explaining the challenge
- include a section clearly explaining judging
- include timeline and rules
- include FAQ
- link to registration form
- use readable layout and accessible contrast

---

## 15. Non-Functional Requirements
- fast load time
- responsive design
- accessible text contrast
- clear typography
- visually polished
- easy scanning with sections and hierarchy
- no cluttered or confusing layout

---

## 16. Design Requirements

**Visual direction:**
- startup launch energy
- modern and slightly futuristic
- strong hierarchy
- polished but not overdesigned

**Possible style ideas:**
- dark theme with bright accent color
- glassmorphism or clean card layout
- subtle gradients
- bold hero typography
- startup/VC aesthetic
- playful microcopy

The page should feel more like "tech launch" than "university noticeboard."

---

## 17. Content Requirements

**Required content blocks:**
- event title
- one-line description
- beginner-friendly note
- what participants build
- AI VC judging concept
- judging criteria
- timeline
- rules
- CTA to register
- FAQ

**Optional content blocks:**
- sample startup ideas
- sample AI investment outcomes like "Rejected" or "Term Sheet"
- judges/prizes
- testimonials from previous events if you have them

---

## 18. Constraints
- audience is beginner-heavy
- event duration is one week
- challenge should sound exciting without sounding too complicated
- page should avoid long dense paragraphs
- the concept must be understandable in under 20 seconds

---

## 19. Risks

**Risk 1: Students do not understand the challenge**

*Mitigation:* Use a very clear "How it works" section with simple steps.

**Risk 2: Students think it is too advanced**

*Mitigation:* Say clearly that they only need to build a landing page, not a full startup.

**Risk 3: Students think the judging is random**

*Mitigation:* Publish clear judging criteria and explain that the AI acts as a VC using those criteria.

**Risk 4: The event feels gimmicky instead of credible**

*Mitigation:* Balance humor with structure. Make the page look polished and organized.

---
